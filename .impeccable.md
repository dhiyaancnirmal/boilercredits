## Design Context

### Users

**Primary**: Current Purdue students, time-pressured, task-oriented. Most common job: "I need to find a class I can take this summer at [local school / online] that counts as [Purdue course X] so I can graduate on time." They arrive with a specific Purdue requirement in mind and want a fast, confident answer. They're in spreadsheet mode — they want dense, scannable data, not a landing page experience.

**Secondary**: Incoming transfer students doing pre-admission planning — more anxious, higher stakes, browsing by school rather than by Purdue course. They need the same clarity but with more context about what each match actually means (distributed vs. undistributed credit, core requirements satisfied).

**Context**: Dorm room late at night, phone in bed, registration window opens in 6 hours. Fast answers matter more than anything. This tool replaces a clunky Purdue web form that makes students fill out cascading dropdowns one field at a time.

### Brand Personality

**3 words**: clear, precise, refined

**Voice**: No hedging. No onboarding copy that restates what the interface already shows. No exclamation points. No "Oops, something went wrong!" The UI should speak the way a trusted advisor does — direct, calm, complete.

**Emotional goal**: Confidence and relief. The student should feel like they have the complete picture and can make a decision right now.

**What this is NOT**: A student project. Not a startup landing page. Not a booster-themed mascot app. Not "another Purdue tool." A tool so well-considered it doesn't feel like it was made by a student even though it was.

### Aesthetic Direction

**Visual tone**: Linear/Vercel dashboard. Dense, dark, quiet. Information fills the screen with purpose. Chrome is minimal — no decorative borders, no cards-within-cards, no gradient fills or glowing accents. Spacing is deliberate and asymmetric. Every divider earns its place by separating genuinely distinct content.

**References**:
- **Linear** — tight typographic hierarchy, dark surfaces, near-zero decoration, keyboard-first density. Information is the design.
- **Vercel dashboard** — clean, robust, the right controls in the right places. Nothing is out of place. No horizontal rules everywhere. Buttons are sized for their importance.
- **Raycast** — premium, instant, focused. Feels like it was made by someone who cares deeply about craft.

**Anti-references**:
- `boilerclasses.com` — centered emoji icon + giant wordmark, too much empty space, student-project aesthetic, generic dark template
- `boilerexams.com` — cartoon mascot illustration, hero/CTA landing page pattern, Tailwind template feel, decorative art that adds noise without aiding the task
- `boilermath.vercel.app` — vibe-coded with no typographic consideration, no visual hierarchy
- Generic "AI dark mode": cyan-on-dark accents, glowing card borders, purple gradients, glassmorphism for its own sake

**Theme**: Dark only. The primary user is working late, cross-referencing their degree plan, making high-stakes decisions under deadline pressure. Dark is correct — not for aesthetics but for context.

**Color**:
- Background: `oklch(12% 0.003 75)` — near-black with the faintest warm tint toward Purdue gold
- Accent: `oklch(78% 0.05 75)` — Purdue wheat/gold, warm, earned. Non-negotiable brand anchor.
- Neutrals: tinted toward hue 75° to create subconscious cohesion with the gold accent
- No pure black (#000) or pure white (#fff)
- No cyan, no purple, no neon

**Typography**:
- **Geist** (Vercel's open-source typeface, Google Fonts) for all UI — directly references the user's stated aesthetic inspiration, clean neo-grotesque, slightly narrower than Inter, strong at small sizes in dark backgrounds
- Single font for the entire site. No secondary mono face.
- Course codes (CS 25100, MA 16100, 1XHUM) use `font-variant-numeric: tabular-nums` on Geist so digits align in columns without switching families.
- No all-caps secondary text. Small secondary labels in sentence case, reduced opacity, not text-transform.
- Scale: tight 5-step scale with clear weight contrast (400/500/600), letter-spacing -0.01 to -0.02em on headers

### Design Principles

1. **Information is the interface.** No decorative chrome. Every border, shadow, background tint, and color exists to separate or emphasize data — not to look designed. If removing an element doesn't lose information, remove it.

2. **Name things by what the student does, not by technical direction.** Tabs, labels, and headings describe the job-to-be-done: "Find a class elsewhere for Purdue credit" — not "From Purdue." Credit codes are decoded in place; the student should never need to open a separate reference page.

3. **Earned density.** The tool should feel capable — packed with information — but not cluttered. Hierarchy through weight, size, and whitespace contrast. Tight groups, generous gaps between sections. Dense like a spreadsheet, legible like a dashboard.

4. **Precision builds trust.** Correct edge-case details (C- minimum, 10-year rule, Mitch Daniels restrictions, quarter-to-semester conversion) shown at the moment they're relevant build more trust than any brand copy. Show the student you know the system better than the system's own website does.

5. **Invisible infrastructure.** Loading, caching, and proxy complexity should be imperceptible. Loading states are brief and honest — a short status pill, not a skeleton screen. Errors are specific. The tool should feel instant because the underlying caching makes it instant.
